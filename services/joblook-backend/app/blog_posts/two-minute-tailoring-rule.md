Most job seekers make the same trade. They send a hundred generic applications, get six replies, and call it a numbers game. Or they spend ninety minutes on every application and burn out after four of them. Both approaches lose.

The middle path is a rule we teach everyone who installs JobLook: spend two minutes — not zero, not ninety — on tailoring. Here is exactly how.

## The two minutes, timed

**Minute one: read the job post twice.**
First pass, highlight every verb the company used to describe the role. "Own", "ship", "partner", "scale", "de-risk". Second pass, find the one line that describes what success looks like in the first six months. That line is your north star for the whole application.

**Minute two: change two things.**
In your answer to "why this role?", include the verb that matters most from minute one. In your resume bullet that's closest to their six-month success line, swap in a metric that proves you've done something like it before. That is the entire tailoring step.

## Why this works

Applications get rejected for one of three reasons. You didn't match the required keywords (the ATS filtered you). You didn't sound like you understood the role (the recruiter filtered you). You sounded identical to everyone else (the hiring manager filtered you).

Two minutes of tailoring addresses the second and third. The first — ATS keywords — is something your resume should already handle at the template level, not per application.

## What most people get wrong

They rewrite the whole cover letter. They add a paragraph explaining their motivation. They try to match the company's tone of voice. None of this moves the needle.

What moves the needle is showing the recruiter, in the first two sentences of your long-form answer, that you read past the job title. One specific detail from the posting — a product they mentioned, a problem they said is hard, a metric in the description — is worth more than three paragraphs of enthusiasm.

## The JobLook version

When you use the extension to draft a custom answer, the AI does the first pass for you: it reads the posting, pulls the specific detail, and weaves in the most relevant metric from your resume. But the draft is just a draft. Your two minutes go into reading it, deleting one sentence that sounds generic, and fixing the one factual claim the AI got slightly wrong.

That edit is the difference between "AI-generated" and "human with AI leverage". Recruiters can tell. So can hiring managers. Don't skip it.

## The simpler version

If you take nothing else from this post: **never submit an application without changing at least one sentence that references something specific to the company.** Not the company name. Something specific — a product, a customer, a recent launch, a public metric, a stated problem.

That single discipline will triple your response rate. Time it. Two minutes. Every application.
